Dover Stone Church Lane

Dover Plains, NY

Deep in the wooded hills of Dover Plains, New York lies a place known as Dover Stone Church. Despite its name, it is not a church at all, but a cavern carved into metamorphic rock. Hidden in the forest and reached only by a narrow path, the formation has drawn visitors for generations. The Town of Dover owns the land where the cavern sits, more than a mile from the nearest highway.

But between the highway and the cavern lies privately owned land. Running through that property is a narrow roadway known as Stone Church Lane, a rustic path that winds through the woods before reaching the Town's parcel. For years, visitors followed this route to reach the cavern, walking through the private property as though it were a public entrance to the natural landmark.

Over time, the steady stream of visitors transformed what had once been quiet woodland into a passageway. Cars arrived, people parked along the road, and groups of hikers passed through the property on their way to the cavern beyond. What many visitors saw as a scenic walk in the woods, the property owner experienced as strangers regularly crossing private land.

In 2018, the landowner turned to the courts. She filed an action in Supreme Court in Dutchess County seeking to establish that the public had no legal right to cross her property to reach Dover Stone Church. Her lawsuit sought to quiet title under RPAPL Article 15 and asked the court to declare that the portion of Stone Church Lane located on her property could not be used by the public as an access route.

The case raised a deeper issue as well. For years, the Town had allowed visitors to travel to the cavern even though the most direct path required crossing private property. The landowner argued that by permitting this use, the Town had effectively turned her land into a public walkway. If that were the case, she argued, then the Town had taken her property for public use without providing compensation.

The dispute moved through the courts and eventually reached the Appellate Division, Second Department. The appellate court examined the legal documents governing the property and the rules of New York property law concerning easements and public rights of way.

Ultimately, the court concluded that the law did not grant the Town the right to invite the general public to cross the private property. Whatever easement may have existed did not authorize public access, and the Town failed to prove that the path had become a public highway through longstanding use.

The appellate court reversed the earlier ruling and directed that judgment be entered declaring that members of the public have no legal right to traverse the portion of Stone Church Lane located on the private property in order to reach Dover Stone Church.

The case, however, did not end there. The court returned the matter to Supreme Court to determine whether the Town's actions over the years — knowingly allowing visitors to cross private land in order to reach the cavern — amounted to a taking of private property for which compensation may be owed.

The cavern remains in the woods where it has stood for centuries. But the quiet path leading to it has become the center of an ongoing legal dispute about the balance between public curiosity and private property rights.
