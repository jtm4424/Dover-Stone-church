Stone Church Lane Dispute

A narrative of the Dover Stone Church access case

A property dispute in the Town of Dover arose over access to a well-known natural landmark called Dover Stone Church. Despite its name, Dover Stone Church is not a church at all, but a large cavern formed within metamorphic rock. The Town of Dover owns the parcel on which the cavern sits. The site lies deep within a wooded area more than a mile from the nearest highway and has long attracted visitors.

Between the highway and the Town’s land lies private property owned by the plaintiff. A path known as Stone Church Lane runs from the highway toward the cavern and passes directly across the plaintiff’s land before reaching the Town’s property. For years, members of the public used this pathway to walk through the plaintiff’s property in order to visit the cavern.

In 2018, the plaintiff commenced an action in Supreme Court, Dutchess County. She alleged that members of the public regularly crossed her property without legal authority and sought relief under RPAPL Article 15 to quiet title. She also requested declaratory and injunctive relief declaring that the public had no right to traverse her land. In addition, she argued that the Town had effectively allowed the public to use her property as an access route to the landmark, which she contended amounted to a de facto taking of her property for public use without just compensation.

The plaintiff moved for summary judgment. She asked the court to declare that the public had no legal right to cross her property via Stone Church Lane and that the Town’s encouragement or allowance of public access constituted a compensable taking. The Town opposed the motion, arguing that the plaintiff had not proven her entitlement to judgment as a matter of law and that further discovery was necessary.

The Supreme Court ruled against the plaintiff in significant respects. The court denied her request for summary judgment and went further. After reviewing the record, the court concluded that a valid easement existed in favor of the Town across the portion of Stone Church Lane located on the plaintiff’s property. Based on that conclusion, the court effectively granted summary judgment to the Town. The court also denied the plaintiff’s claim that the public’s use of her property constituted a taking. In addition, the court took the unusual step of issuing directives on its own initiative: it imposed certain time and date restrictions on the public’s use of the path and ordered the parties to develop a management plan governing access. If the parties could not agree on such a plan, the court stated that it would devise one itself.

The plaintiff appealed.

The Appellate Division, Second Department, reversed the order. The appellate court explained that an easement appurtenant must be created in writing by the property owner and must burden one parcel of land for the benefit of another. The court further noted the established rule that a reservation in a deed in favor of a third party who is not part of the conveyance, a “stranger to the deed,” does not create a valid property interest for that third party.

Applying these principles, the appellate court concluded that the plaintiff had demonstrated her entitlement to judgment as a matter of law. The evidence showed that the easement affecting her property did not grant the Town the right to allow or invite the general public to cross her land. The Town failed to produce evidence sufficient to raise a triable issue of fact. The Town’s additional argument, that the path had become a public highway by long use under Highway Law § 189, also failed because the Town did not present proof sufficient to support that claim.

Because the Town did not establish any legal right allowing the public to traverse the plaintiff’s property, the Supreme Court should have granted the plaintiff’s motion declaring that the public had no right of access across her land. The appellate court also held that the lower court erred when it searched the record and awarded summary judgment to the Town. Since the existence of an easement was not supported by the law or the evidence, the directives issued by the Supreme Court regarding time restrictions and access management were also improper.

The Appellate Division therefore reversed the order and directed that judgment be entered declaring that members of the general public have no right to cross the portion of Stone Church Lane located on the plaintiff’s property in order to reach Dover Stone Church.

The appellate court did not, however, finally resolve the plaintiff’s claim for compensation. It determined that the Supreme Court should not have rejected the plaintiff’s de facto taking claim without first conducting a hearing on the merits. The case was therefore sent back to Supreme Court, Dutchess County, for further proceedings. The lower court must now hold a hearing, and possibly permit additional discovery, to determine whether the Town’s conduct in allowing public access amounted to a taking of the plaintiff’s property for which just compensation is owed.

In short, the appellate court ruled that the public has no legal right to cross the plaintiff’s land to reach Dover Stone Church and directed that judgment be entered accordingly, while leaving open the question of whether the Town’s prior conduct requires it to compensate the plaintiff for the public’s use of her property.
